Pharmaceutical Analysis

Welcome to the Pharmaceutical Analysis page! Here you’ll find questions and answers to help you master this area.

Questions and Answers

  • 1a) What is autoxidation? Explain the mechanism of drug autoxidation (5mks)
  • Autoxidation is a process where substances react with oxygen spontaneously. It often leads to the degradation of drugs, particularly those with double bonds. The mechanism involves initiation (formation of free radicals), propagation (reaction with oxygen to form peroxides), and termination (stabilization of radicals).
  • 1b) Explain factors that affect the autoxidation process (5mks)
  • Factors affecting autoxidation include light exposure, temperature, pH, presence of metal ions, and oxygen concentration. These factors can accelerate or slow down the rate of oxidation.
  • 2a) How can drug oxidation during storage be minimized? (5mks)
  • Drug oxidation can be minimized by storing drugs in airtight containers, using antioxidants, maintaining low temperatures, protecting from light, and using inert gases to reduce oxygen exposure.
  • 2b) Explain isomerization as a means of drug degradation (5mks)
  • Isomerization is the process by which a drug molecule changes its structural form, creating an isomer. This change can reduce the drug's efficacy or produce harmful effects. An example is the conversion of cis to trans forms in certain drugs.
  • 3a) When antiseptic formaldehyde solution is stored in the cold, a white precipitate appears. Explain why. (3mks)
  • The white precipitate is likely due to polymerization of formaldehyde, forming paraformaldehyde. This reaction is temperature-dependent and more likely to occur at lower temperatures.
  • 3b) Using a chemical equation, show how procaine undergoes hydrolysis and then decarboxylation.
  • Procaine (C13H20N2O2) hydrolyzes to produce p-aminobenzoic acid and diethylaminoethanol, followed by decarboxylation of p-aminobenzoic acid.
    Equation: C13H20N2O2 + H2O → C9H11NO2 + C4H11NO
  • 4) Explain physical degradation of drugs, citing examples in each case. (10mks)
  • Physical degradation includes processes like melting, evaporation, and sublimation. Examples: volatile drugs evaporate easily, while drugs like insulin lose potency due to temperature changes.
  • 5a) Differentiate between Sedatives and Hypnotics. (2mks)
  • Sedatives are drugs that reduce irritability and excitement, while hypnotics are drugs that induce and maintain sleep.
  • 5b) State 4 chemical groups of sedatives & hypnotics and give one drug example in each case. (8mks)
  • Chemical groups:
    1. Benzodiazepines (e.g., Diazepam)
    2. Barbiturates (e.g., Phenobarbital)
    3. Non-benzodiazepine hypnotics (e.g., Zolpidem)
    4. Melatonin receptor agonists (e.g., Ramelteon)
  • 6) Discuss the SAR of sedatives–hypnotic drugs given the general structure. (10mks)
  • The Structure-Activity Relationship (SAR) for sedatives-hypnotics shows that ring size and substituent position affect activity. Electron-donating groups enhance sedative effects, while halogens improve potency.
  • 7a) Discuss the SAR of benzodiazepines as sedative-hypnotic drugs. (6mks)
  • Benzodiazepines' SAR reveals that modifications to the ring system and addition of electron-donating groups increase potency and duration of sedative effects.

  • 8a) Explain the utility of radioisotopes in medicine and science. (6mks)
  • Radioisotopes are used in medical diagnostics (e.g., iodine-131 for thyroid imaging) and in cancer therapy, as they can target specific areas within the body.
  • 8b) State 4 properties of radioisotopes that govern their choice when used for therapy. (4mks)
  • Properties: 1. Half-life 2. Type of radiation emitted 3. Energy of radiation 4. Biocompatibility
  • 9) Classify antiepileptics into various chemical groups, giving general structure in each case. (10mks)
  • Antiepileptics classification:
    1. Barbiturates (e.g., Phenobarbital)
    2. Hydantoins (e.g., Phenytoin)
    3. Succinimides (e.g., Ethosuximide)
