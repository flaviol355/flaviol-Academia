Questions

  • 1. What is the normality of H2SO4 acid labeled 25% w/v solution?
    Answer: The normality is approximately 5.1 N.
  • 2. If 500 ml of a 15% v/v solution are diluted to 500 ml, what will be the percentage strength (v/v)?
    Answer: The percentage strength remains 15% v/v.
  • 3. How many milliliters of water should be added to 80 ml of a 20% w/w aqueous solution to prepare a 5% w/w solution?
    Answer: 240 ml of water should be added.
  • 4. How many milliliters of a 1:400 w/v stock solution should be used to make 4 liters of a 1:2000 w/v solution?
    Answer: 800 ml of the stock solution is needed.
  • 5. How many grams of zinc oxide should be added to 3200 g of 5% zinc oxide ointment to prepare an ointment containing 20% of zinc oxide?
    Answer: 600 g of zinc oxide should be added.
  • 6. What is the percentage strength of alcohol in a mixture of 500 ml of a solution containing 40% v/v alcohol, 400 ml of a second solution containing 21% v/v alcohol, and a sufficient quantity of non-alcoholic third solution to make a total of 1000 ml?
    Answer: The percentage strength of alcohol in the mixture is 28.4% v/v.
  • 7. A hospital ward uses a particular disinfectant for three different purposes, a 4% w/v solution is used as a floor disinfectant, a 9000 ppm disinfectant is used to disinfect surgical instruments, and a 1:500 w/v solution is used as a skin antiseptic. Calculate and explain how 2 liters of each of the solutions may be prepared from a 45% w/v stock solution.
    Answer:
    • For 4% w/v solution: 178 ml of stock solution and add water to make 2 liters.
    • For 9000 ppm solution: Use 18 g of the stock solution and add water to make 2 liters.
    • For 1:500 w/v solution: Use 4 ml of stock solution and add water to make 2 liters.
  • 8. Calculate the amount of base to be added to 150 g of 10% w/w salicylic acid ointment so as to produce a 6% w/w salicylic acid ointment.
    Answer: 100 g of base should be added.
  • 9. A solution contains 10 mg/100 ml of Ca ions. Express this concentration in terms of milliequivalents per liter.
    Answer: 2.5 mEq/L.

Section B

  • 1. What are the advantages of powders as a dosage form?

    Powders as a dosage form offer the following advantages:

    • Easy to administer and swallow, especially for patients with difficulty swallowing pills.
    • Faster dissolution rate, leading to quicker absorption and onset of action.
    • Allow for larger doses of medication, which may be challenging in tablet or capsule form.
    • Greater stability for certain drugs compared to liquid forms.
    • Customization is possible, allowing pharmacists to prepare doses tailored to individual patients.
  • 2. Differentiate between divided and undivided powders.

    Differences between divided and undivided powders:

    • Divided powders: Pre-measured into individual doses, typically packaged in paper or plastic packets.
    • Undivided powders: Provided in bulk, with patients required to measure each dose themselves.
  • 3. Describe doubling-up method.

    The doubling-up method is a compounding technique where a small portion of drug is mixed with an equal amount of excipient. This mixture is then doubled repeatedly until the desired total quantity is reached, ensuring a uniform blend.

  • 4. Discuss advantages of capsules as a dosage form.

    Capsules offer several advantages:

    • Mask unpleasant tastes or odors of drugs.
    • Easy to swallow and administer.
    • Provide dose uniformity, especially for powders and granules.
    • Protect sensitive drugs from degradation in the stomach.
    • Allow for modified-release formulations to control drug release over time.
  • 5. Discuss two methods of filling capsules.

    Two common methods of capsule filling are:

    • Manual filling: Used in small-scale operations; involves filling capsules by hand with the required dose.
    • Automatic filling: Uses machines, including tamping and auger methods, for precise and efficient dosing on a large scale.
  • 6. What are the essential qualities of a good tablet?

    Good tablets should have the following qualities:

    • Appropriate hardness and strength to withstand handling without breaking.
    • Low friability to minimize powdering during handling and storage.
    • Rapid disintegration to release the drug promptly.
    • Pleasant taste or coating to mask any unpleasant flavors.
    • Stability to maintain potency over time.
    • Uniformity in weight and dose to ensure consistent efficacy.
  • 7. Discuss the ideal properties of an enteric coating material.

    An ideal enteric coating material should have these properties:

    • Resistant to gastric acid to prevent breakdown in the stomach.
    • Dissolves in the less acidic environment of the intestines, allowing targeted drug release.
    • Non-toxic and safe for consumption.
    • Provides a protective layer for drugs sensitive to stomach acid.
  • 8. Discuss the stages of sugar coating.

    The stages of sugar coating a tablet include:

    • Sealing: Initial coating to protect the drug from moisture.
    • Subcoating: Builds up the tablet size and rounds its edges.
    • Smoothing: Provides a smoother surface for even coloring.
    • Coloring: Adds color for appearance and branding.
    • Polishing: Gives the tablet a shiny, finished look.
    • Printing: Optional stage where the tablet is printed with identifiers.
