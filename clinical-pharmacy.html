Questions

  • Caroline, an 8-month-old girl presents to a health facility with mucous clogged nostrils and fever. Lab investigations rule out the possibility of bacterial infections. Design possible safe approaches for her management. (5 marks)

    Answer:

    • Saline nasal drops to clear the nostrils.
    • Use of a humidifier in the room to ease breathing.
    • Encouraging fluid intake to prevent dehydration.
    • Monitoring the fever and providing acetaminophen if necessary (appropriate for her age and dosage).
    • Regularly cleaning her nose with a soft, damp cloth.

  • Define the term arrhythmia. (2 marks)

    Answer: Arrhythmia is an abnormality in the rhythm of the heart, which can be too fast (tachycardia), too slow (bradycardia), or irregular (such as in atrial fibrillation).

  • Describe 5 drug classes used as antiarrhythmics, indicating their effects on the cardiac action potentials. (4 marks)

    Answer:

    • Class I (Sodium channel blockers): Reduces the speed of conduction in the heart, useful in ventricular arrhythmias.
    • Class II (Beta-blockers): Slows down heart rate and reduces the strength of heart muscle contraction.
    • Class III (Potassium channel blockers): Prolongs repolarization, effective for arrhythmias like atrial fibrillation.
    • Class IV (Calcium channel blockers): Decreases conduction through the AV node, useful for arrhythmias originating in the atria.
    • Class V (Miscellaneous agents, e.g., Digoxin): Increases vagal tone, reducing heart rate, especially in atrial fibrillation.

  • Giving reason, explain a safe remedy for each of the following (5 marks):
    • Epiglottitis: Antibiotics and steroids to reduce inflammation and treat infection, as epiglottitis is often caused by bacterial infection.
    • Laryngitis: Resting the voice and steam inhalation to soothe the larynx, as laryngitis is usually viral or due to overuse.
    • Tonsillitis: Acetaminophen or ibuprofen for pain relief, and antibiotics if bacterial (e.g., streptococcal) infection is confirmed.
    • Tracheaitis: Antibiotics, if bacterial infection is suspected, and humidified air to ease breathing.
    • Bronchitis: Drinking plenty of fluids and rest, as bronchitis is often viral; inhalers or bronchodilators may be used if wheezing is present.
  • Murfie is a 50-year-old patient who presents at a hospital outpatient section with complaints of chest pain, fatigue, strained breathing, and a cough of more than 2 months. Expiratory flow rates prove to be less than normal and had negligible improvement with salbutamol inhalation. He has been a cigarette smoker since his early 20s. Giving a possible diagnosis, describe possible approaches for his management. (20 marks)

    Answer:

    • Diagnosis: Likely chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) due to long-term smoking.
    • Management Approaches:
      • Smoking cessation programs to prevent further lung damage.
      • Prescription of long-acting bronchodilators (e.g., tiotropium) if short-acting ones have been ineffective.
      • Inhaled corticosteroids to reduce airway inflammation.
      • Pulmonary rehabilitation to improve breathing and physical capacity.
      • Vaccinations (influenza and pneumococcal) to prevent infections.
      • Regular follow-up to monitor lung function and adjust treatments.

  • Douglas, a 10-year-old pupil, complains of having stuffy nostrils, one-sided facial pain, and a forehead headache for 8 days. He claims that his symptoms are only getting worse and that his nasal secretions are cream white and much more than the first 5 days. Suggest 5 most possible remedies. (5 marks)

    Answer:

    • Saline nasal irrigation to clear mucus and reduce congestion.
    • Steam inhalation to relieve nasal congestion.
    • Nasal decongestant sprays for temporary relief (should be used sparingly to avoid rebound congestion).
    • Analgesics (e.g., acetaminophen or ibuprofen) for pain and headache relief.
    • Consultation with a physician for possible antibiotic treatment if a bacterial sinus infection is suspected.
