Questions

  • 1. State 3 advantages and 2 disadvantages of Fixed Dose Combinations.
    • Advantages: Improved adherence, reduced pill burden, cost-effective treatment.
    • Disadvantages: Limited dosing flexibility, risk of overdose if one component is not required.
  • 2. State 5 principles of antimicrobial therapy.
    • Selective toxicity, appropriate drug selection, correct dose, proper duration, combination therapy when necessary.
  • 3. State the mechanism of action of co-trimoxazole (septrin).
    • Inhibits bacterial folate synthesis by blocking dihydropteroate synthase (sulfamethoxazole) and dihydrofolate reductase (trimethoprim).
  • 4. List 5 major indications of clindamycin.
    • Skin and soft tissue infections, bone and joint infections, intra-abdominal infections, dental infections, and pneumonia caused by susceptible bacteria.
  • 5. State four indications of amphotericin-B.
    • Treatment of systemic fungal infections, cryptococcal meningitis, leishmaniasis, aspergillosis.
  • 6. State the advantage of the following combinations:
    • a) Ampicillin + cloxacillin (ampiclox): Broader antibacterial spectrum covering both Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria.
    • b) Sulfamethoxazole + trimethoprim (co-trimoxazole): Synergistic effect in inhibiting bacterial folate synthesis.
    • c) Imipenem + cilastatin: Cilastatin prevents the degradation of imipenem in the kidneys, enhancing its antibacterial effect.
    • d) Ticarcillin + aminoglycosides: Broader spectrum against Gram-negative organisms, especially Pseudomonas.
    • e) Rifampicin + isoniazid + pyrazinamide (Rifater): Combination for effective tuberculosis treatment and to prevent resistance.

Advanced Pharmacology Questions

This section provides a deeper dive into complex pharmacology concepts.

  • 1. State 5 principles of antimicrobial therapy. (5 marks)
    • Selective toxicity
    • Appropriate drug selection
    • Correct dose
    • Proper duration
    • Combination therapy when necessary
  • 2. State 5 adverse drug reactions of insulin. (5 marks)
    • Hypoglycemia
    • Weight gain
    • Allergic reactions
    • Insulin resistance
    • Lipodystrophy at the injection site
  • 3. Outline the mechanism of action of NSAIDs. (5 marks)
    • NSAIDs inhibit the cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes, COX-1 and COX-2, which are responsible for the formation of prostaglandins. By inhibiting prostaglandin synthesis, NSAIDs reduce inflammation, pain, and fever.
  • 4. State the mechanism of action of any 5 classes of antihypertensives. (5 marks)
    • ACE inhibitors: Block the conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II, leading to vasodilation.
    • Beta-blockers: Reduce heart rate and myocardial contractility by blocking beta-adrenergic receptors.
    • Calcium channel blockers: Inhibit calcium ions from entering cardiac and smooth muscle cells, causing vasodilation.
    • Diuretics: Increase renal excretion of sodium and water, reducing blood volume.
    • Angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARBs): Block angiotensin II receptors, leading to vasodilation and reduced aldosterone secretion.
